Lesson 2: Plate Boundaries

Gear Up for Learning!

Plate tectonics is a theory that explains the Earth's surface is broken into large and small lithospheric plates, which are moving slowly. The size and position of these plates change over time. They move against each other at their edges, causing intense geologic activity, such as earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain building.

In your previous lesson, you covered Plate tectonics and its relationship to the distribution of earthquake epicenters, active volcanoes, and major mountain belts.

This lesson will provide you with information and activities that will help you understand Plate Boundaries (Divergent, Convergent, and Transform faults).

After this lesson, you should be able to describe the different types of plate boundaries (S10 -Ia -j-36.2).

Build Your Knowledge

As you have learned in the previous lesson, the lithospheric plates rests on top of the asthenosphere and moves at a rate between 1 and 10 cm per year. The tectonic plates move with respect to each other in three ways. The tectonic plates may move apart (diverge), toward each other (converge), or past each other. The regions where the tectonic plates meet are aptly called plate boundaries.

Divergent Plate Boundary

When two plates move away from each other, a gap or rift between them is created. As the plates move away, the space between them is filled with magma that forms underneath.

Molten lava rises, cools, and becomes part of the ocean floor. If the East African Rift Zone gap eventually widens, it then evolves into a rift valley. Divergent boundaries between oceanic plates produce mid-oceanic ridge, a mountain system that is formed on the seafloor.

Any continent or landmass above a divergent plate boundary will be torn apart into distinct landmasses. The surrounding body of water will fill the space between them and later becomes an ocean. The East African Rift Valley is an example of this phenomenon. Millions of years from now, East Africa and West Africa may move apart and become separated by an ocean.

Convergent Plate Boundary

Convergent boundaries occur when two plates slide toward each other and form either a subduction zone (one plate moves underneath the other) or an orogenic belt. An orogenic belt is a region of deformed rocks that is formed when two plates collide and one plate crumples and is uplifted. What happens at the boundary depends on the nature of the colliding plates. There are three kinds of convergence: oceanic-oceanic, oceanic-continental, and continental-continental convergence.

When two oceanic plates collide, a subduction zone is formed. The descending oceanic plate begins to melt upon contact with the asthenosphere. The molten material begins to rise, creating a chain of volcanoes. Through time, volcanic debris from the eruption of underwater or submarine volcanoes pile up until an island volcano rises above sea level. The interaction of the Pacific Plate and the Philippine Plate is an example of an oceanic-oceanic convergence. A long, narrow depression in the ocean floor called oceanic trench is also created at the subduction zone. A chain of volcanoes may also form parallel to the convergent boundary. The Mariana Trench in the western Pacific Ocean is formed by this type of convergence.

Marianas Trench

Figure 2.2 The convergence of two oceanic plates forms volcanic islands and underwater volcanoes.

In an oceanic-continental subduction, the denser oceanic plate moved underneath the lighter continental plate. As the oceanic plate descends into the mantle, the temperature increases, causing the rocks to melt and form magma. This hot and less dense magma will eventually seek a vent, which in most cases are existing volcanoes, to reach the surface. The edges of the Pacific Plate are subduction zones; hence, chains of volcanoes are found above it. The Andes Mountains in South America and the Cascade Range in North America are formed by this type of convergence.

Andres Mountain Range

Figure 2.3 Oceanic – continental convergence pushes the continental plate upwards creating volcanoes.

When two continental plates collide, no subduction happens. Instead, the continents suture themselves. The continental crust buckles and rocks pile up, creating mountain ranges. The Alps in Europe is formed from the convergence of the African Plate and Eurasian Plate, and the Himalayas in Asia is the result of the collision between the Indian Plate and Eurasian Plate.

Alps Mountain Range

Figure 2.4 Continental – continental convergence creates mountain ranges.

Transform Plate Boundary

In transform plate boundaries, the plates slide against each other in opposite directions. No mountains or volcanoes are produced. Instead, a massive amount of energy is released, causing earthquakes.

An earthquake refers to the shaking of Earth as a result of the breaking or shifting of the rocks of the tectonic plates, which releases seismic energy. A majority of earthquakes occur along the boundaries of tectonic plates, which either collide, push, or slide past each other. Earthquakes occur because of the pressure underneath Earth's crust that pushes blocks of rocks to move along fault lines.

Figure 2.5 Types of faults

Faults are cracks on Earth's crust, of which there are three types-normal fault, reverse fault, and strike-slip fault. The surface where the blocks slip past each other is called the fault plane.

Dip-slip refers to the movement of faults along the angle of the fault plane. The angle of movement is called the dip, while the movement is the slip. Dip slips are classified as normal fault or reverse fault. A normal fault is a dip-slip fault where a block that sits on a fault plane, called the banging wall, slips downward with respect to the footwall along the dip angle. Reverse fault is another type of dip-slip fault where the block of Earth's crust pushes upward and along the dip angle. Thrust fault is a type of reverse fault where the angle of the dip is 45° or less.

In a strike-slip fault, nearly vertical fault planes or surfaces slide in parallel but opposite directions. The San Andreas Fault in California, USA is an example. The plates move in opposite directions and, when the plates get stuck and stop moving, energy builds up between them. Energy reverberates through the crust and mantle, releasing seismic waves that are felt on the surface as earthquakes.

Oblique-slip fault is a combination of a strike-slip and a dip-slip. Blocks of Earth's crust slide horizontally (similar to a strike-slip) and obliquely up or down from each other (like a dip-slip).

Connect the Dots

▲

Now that you have explored the different types of plate boundaries and the various movements that occur within Earth's crust, it's time to reflect on the key concepts you have learned. This summary will help reinforce your understanding of how plate movements shape our planet, leading to the formation of geological features such as mountains, volcanoes, rift valleys, and ocean trenches, as well as natural events like earthquakes.

  • The lithospheric plates rest on the asthenosphere and move at a rate of 1–10 cm per year.
  • Plate boundaries are regions where tectonic plates meet and interact in three main ways: divergent, convergent, and transform boundaries.

Divergent Plate Boundaries

  • Occur when two plates move apart, creating a rift or gap.
  • Magma rises to fill the gap, cools, and forms new oceanic crust.
  • These boundaries form features like mid-ocean ridges (e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge).
  • On land, divergence can lead to the formation of rift valleys, such as the East African Rift Valley.
  • Over millions of years, rift valleys may become new ocean basins.

Convergent Plate Boundaries

  • Form when two plates move toward each other, resulting in either subduction zones or orogenic belts.
  • Oceanic-oceanic convergence creates subduction zones, volcanic island arcs, and trenches (e.g., Mariana Trench).
  • Oceanic-continental convergence causes the oceanic plate to subduct, forming volcano chains like the Andes Mountains.
  • Continental-continental convergence leads to mountain building without subduction, forming ranges such as the Himalayas and the Alps.

Transform Plate Boundaries

  • Occur when plates slide past each other horizontally.
  • Do not produce volcanoes or mountains, but cause earthquakes due to built-up energy release.
  • The San Andreas Fault in California is a well-known transform boundary.

Faults and Earthquakes

  • Faults are cracks in the Earth's crust where movement occurs.
  • Types of faults:
    • Normal Fault – hanging wall moves downward due to tension.
    • Reverse Fault – hanging wall moves upward due to compression.
    • Strike-slip Fault – blocks move horizontally past each other.
    • Oblique-slip Fault – combines vertical and horizontal movement.
  • Fault planes are the surfaces along which movement occurs.
  • Earthquakes are caused by sudden movements along these faults, releasing seismic energy.
