Lesson 3: Processes and Landforms Along Plate Boundaries

Gear Up for Learning!

Have you ever wondered why earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountains often form in specific regions of the world? These natural events are not random—they are closely related to the movement of huge sections of the Earth’s crust known as tectonic plates. In this lesson, you will explore the different processes that occur along plate boundaries, where these plates meet and interact. Understanding these processes will help you explain how features like trenches, volcanoes, rift valleys, and mountain ranges are formed. This knowledge is essential for us in the Philippines, a country located in one of the most geologically active regions on Earth.

The Earth's lithosphere is broken into several large and small tectonic plates that are constantly in motion. The interactions and processes that occur at the boundaries where these plates meet are fundamental to many geological phenomena. There are three main types of plate boundaries, each characterized by distinct processes:

Figure 3.1 The three types of plate boundaries with their corresponding processes

1. Convergent Boundaries ➡️⬅️ (Destructive Margins)

At convergent boundaries, two plates move towards each other. The specific processes depend on the types of crust involved (oceanic or continental).

  • a. Oceanic-Continental Convergence:
    • Process: When an oceanic plate collides with a continental plate, the denser oceanic plate subducts (sinks) beneath the less dense continental plate.
    • Key Features & Processes:
      • Subduction Zone: The area where the oceanic plate descends into the mantle.
      • Ocean Trench: A deep, linear depression forms on the ocean floor parallel to the subducting plate (e.g., the Mariana Trench). This is the topographic expression of the subduction zone.
      • Volcanic Arc (Continental Arc): As the subducting oceanic plate descends, it heats up and releases water and other volatiles. These volatiles lower the melting point of the overlying mantle wedge, causing it to melt. This magma rises to the surface, erupting to form a chain of volcanoes along the edge of the continental plate (e.g., the Andes Mountains).
      • Earthquakes: Intense seismic activity occurs along the subducting slab (Benioff Zone). These can be shallow, intermediate, or deep-focus earthquakes, and are often very powerful.
      • Accretionary Wedge: Sediments scraped off the subducting oceanic plate and the continental margin accumulate and deform on the overriding continental plate.
      • Mountain Building: The collision and compression can lead to folding and faulting of the continental crust, contributing to mountain building.
  • b. Oceanic-Oceanic Convergence:
    • Process: When two oceanic plates collide, the older, colder, and denser plate subducts beneath the younger, warmer, and less dense plate.
    • Key Features & Processes:
      • Subduction Zone: Similar to oceanic-continental convergence.
      • Ocean Trench: A deep trench is formed (e.g., the Tonga Trench).
      • Volcanic Island Arc: Magma generated from the melting of the mantle wedge (due to volatiles from the subducting plate) rises and erupts on the ocean floor. Over time, these submarine volcanoes can grow to form a chain of volcanic islands (e.g., the Aleutian Islands, Japan, the Philippines).
      • Earthquakes: Frequent and powerful earthquakes occur, similar to oceanic-continental convergence, along the Benioff Zone.
  • c. Continental-Continental Convergence:
    • Process: When two continental plates collide, neither plate is dense enough to subduct significantly into the mantle. Instead, the continental crust buckles, folds, and faults, leading to extensive uplift.
    • Key Features & Processes:
      • Mountain Building (Orogeny): This type of collision forms vast and high mountain ranges (e.g., the Himalayas, formed by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates; the Alps).
      • Crustal Thickening: The crust in the collision zone becomes significantly thicker.
      • Earthquakes: Frequent and powerful shallow to intermediate-focus earthquakes occur due to the immense compressional forces.
      • Little to No Volcanism: Unlike other convergent boundaries, there is generally no significant volcanism because there is no subducting oceanic plate to release volatiles and induce mantle melting in the same way. Any magma present is typically due to crustal melting from the intense pressure and heat.
      • Metamorphism: Intense pressure and heat cause widespread metamorphism of rocks.

2. Divergent Boundaries ⬅️➡️ (Constructive Margins)

At divergent boundaries, two plates move away from each other.

  • a. Oceanic Divergence (Seafloor Spreading):
    • Process: This primarily occurs at mid-ocean ridges. As plates pull apart, tensional stress causes the lithosphere to fracture. Hot asthenospheric mantle material rises to fill the gap.
    • Key Features & Processes:
      • Mid-Ocean Ridges: Long, underwater mountain ranges are formed (e.g., the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East Pacific Rise). These are sites of active volcanism.
      • Rift Valley: A central valley often runs along the crest of the mid-ocean ridge.
      • Seafloor Spreading: New oceanic crust is continuously created as magma rises, cools, and solidifies at the ridge. This new crust is then pushed away from the ridge as more material upwells. This process is a key piece of evidence for plate tectonics.
      • Volcanism: Effusive (non-explosive) basaltic volcanism occurs as magma erupts onto the seafloor, often forming pillow lavas.
      • Earthquakes: Shallow, relatively low-magnitude earthquakes are common along the ridge system due to the tensional forces and magma movement.
      • Hydrothermal Vents: Seawater percolates through cracks in the new crust, is heated by magma, and then re-emerges as hot, mineral-rich plumes, supporting unique ecosystems.

Figure 3.2 Example of a hydrothermal vent

  • b. Continental Divergence (Rifting):
    • Process: When a divergent boundary develops within a continent, the continental crust is stretched and thinned.
    • Key Features & Processes:
      • Rift Valley Formation: The tensional forces cause the crust to fracture and drop down along normal faults, creating a large, elongated depression called a rift valley (e.g., the East African Rift Valley).
      • Volcanism: As the crust thins, magma can rise from the mantle and erupt, leading to volcanic activity within and along the rift. This can include both basaltic flows and more explosive silicic eruptions if continental crust melts.
      • Earthquakes: Shallow earthquakes are common along the faults within the rift system.
      • Formation of New Ocean Basins: If rifting continues, the continental crust can stretch and thin to the point where it breaks apart completely. Seawater can flood the rift valley, eventually leading to the formation of a new linear sea and, ultimately, a new ocean basin with a mid-ocean ridge at its center (e.g., the Red Sea).

Figure 3.3 Formation of new ocean basins

3. Transform Boundaries ⬆️⬇️ (Conservative Margins)

At transform boundaries, two plates slide horizontally past each other. Lithosphere is neither created nor destroyed.

  • Process: The motion is predominantly horizontal, driven by the differential movement of adjacent plates.
  • Key Features & Processes:
    • Transform Faults: These are large-scale strike-slip faults that accommodate the lateral movement between plates (e.g., the San Andreas Fault in California, the Alpine Fault in New Zealand).
    • Earthquakes: As the plates grind past each other, immense stress builds up due to friction. When this stress is suddenly released, it causes powerful, often shallow-focus earthquakes. The longer the plates remain locked, the more stress accumulates, potentially leading to larger earthquakes.
    • No Significant Volcanism: Typically, there is no widespread volcanism associated with transform boundaries because there is no subduction to induce mantle melting and no upwelling of magma as seen at divergent boundaries.
    • Linear Valleys and Ridges: The fault zone can create linear valleys, offset stream channels, and pressure ridges due to the shearing motion and local compression or extension along irregularities in the fault.
    • Crustal Deformation: While crust isn't created or destroyed on a large scale, rocks along the fault zone can be intensely fractured, sheared, and metamorphosed due to the immense frictional forces.

Understanding these processes at plate boundaries is crucial for explaining the distribution of earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain ranges, and other major geological features on Earth. It also helps in assessing and mitigating natural hazards associated with these dynamic zones.

Connect the Dots

▲

Earth’s lithosphere is divided into tectonic plates whose interactions at boundaries drive much of the planet’s geological activity. This section summarizes the three main types of plate boundaries—convergent, divergent, and transform—and their distinct features, processes, and effects. Understanding these boundaries helps explain the patterns of earthquakes, volcanism, and mountain building critical for both science and safety.

  • The Earth’s lithosphere is divided into tectonic plates that are constantly moving.
  • The boundaries where these plates interact are zones of significant geological activity
  • There are three major types of plate boundaries, each with distinct features and processes:

Convergent Boundaries (Destructive Margins) – Plates move toward each other

  • Oceanic-Continental Convergence:
    • Denser oceanic plate subducts beneath continental plate.
    • Forms trenches, continental volcanic arcs, earthquakes, and mountains.
  • Oceanic-Oceanic Convergence:
    • Older, denser oceanic plate subducts.
    • Creates trenches, volcanic island arcs, and frequent earthquakes.
  • Continental-Continental Convergence:
    • No subduction; plates collide and uplift.
    • Leads to mountain building, crustal thickening, shallow earthquakes, and metamorphism.
    • Generally no volcanism.

Divergent Boundaries (Constructive Margins) – Plates move away from each other

  • Oceanic Divergence (Seafloor Spreading):
    • Occurs at mid-ocean ridges; new crust is formed.
    • Features include rift valleys, basaltic volcanism, shallow earthquakes, and hydrothermal vents.
  • Continental Divergence (Rifting):
    • Crust stretches and breaks, forming rift valleys.
    • May result in volcanic activity, earthquakes, and eventually new ocean basins (e.g., Red Sea).

Transform Boundaries (Conservative Margins) – Plates slide past each other

  • No crust is created or destroyed.
  • Characterized by transform faults (e.g., San Andreas Fault).
  • Produces powerful shallow earthquakes, linear valleys, offset landforms, and crustal deformation.
  • Typically no volcanism.
  • These plate boundary processes explain the distribution of volcanoes, earthquakes, and mountain ranges on Earth.
  • Understanding them is essential for disaster preparedness, geological mapping, and earth science education.
